Information theory, founded by Claude Shannon in his landmark 1948 paper "A Mathematical Theory of Communication," transformed how we understand the act of communicating. At its heart lies a deceptively simple question: what is the fundamental limit of compression for a given source of information? Shannon's answer, expressed through the source coding theorem, established that the entropy of a source, measured in bits per symbol, is both an upper bound on what can be compressed away and a lower bound on what must be transmitted to faithfully reconstruct the original.

The entropy of a discrete random source X with probability distribution p is defined as H of X equals the negative sum of p of x times the logarithm base two of p of x, summed over all possible symbols x. This formula captures something deep about randomness and predictability. When all outcomes are equally likely, entropy is maximized. When one outcome dominates the distribution, entropy approaches zero. A fair coin flip has entropy of exactly one bit per flip, while a heavily biased coin carries much less information per toss.

Shannon's source coding theorem states that for any source with entropy H, there exists a uniquely decodable code whose average codeword length L satisfies H less than or equal to L, which is less than H plus one. Furthermore, no uniquely decodable code can achieve average length below H. This bracketing result is remarkable for two reasons. First, entropy is not merely a convenient mathematical quantity, but a physical limit on the compression of information. Second, even simple constructions, such as the one Shannon himself proposed, can approach this optimum to within a single bit per symbol.

Shannon's original construction proceeds in five steps. First, compute the probability of each symbol from the source statistics. Second, sort the symbols in decreasing order of probability. Third, compute cumulative probabilities F sub i, defined as the sum of probabilities of all symbols ranked strictly above the current one. Fourth, assign each symbol a codeword length L sub i equal to the ceiling of the negative logarithm base two of its probability. Finally, take the binary expansion of F sub i and use its first L sub i bits as the codeword. The resulting code is a prefix code, meaning no codeword is a prefix of another, which guarantees instantaneous decoding from a continuous bit stream without lookahead.

In practice, Shannon's code is rarely the optimal choice. Huffman coding, discovered by David Huffman in 1952 while a graduate student at MIT, produces codes that achieve the minimum possible average length among all prefix codes for a given distribution. Yet Shannon's original construction retains pedagogical value. It directly exposes the connection between probabilities and codeword lengths, and it serves as a stepping stone toward arithmetic coding, the modern workhorse of statistical compression that unifies coding and probabilistic modeling into a single elegant framework.
